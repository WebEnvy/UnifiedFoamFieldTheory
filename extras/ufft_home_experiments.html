01

Find a Stone's Voice

Discover the eigenfrequency of any stone empirically — the same way ancient masons did.

Every stone rings at its natural frequency when struck. This experiment teaches you to find it, measure it, and match it with a speaker. This is the foundational skill for everything else.

🛒 Equipment

  • Stones of various sizes (20cm+) Free
  • Smartphone with spectrum analyser app Free
  • Small hammer or metal rod ~$5
  • Bluetooth speaker or powered speaker Existing
  • Tone generator app (phone/laptop) Free

📱 Apps Needed

  • Spectroid (Android) or SpectrumView (iOS) — shows frequency spectrum in real time
  • Function Generator app — produces precise sine wave tones at any frequency
  • Both are free.
1

Strike and Listen

Place your stone on a soft surface (towel, grass). Tap it firmly with the hammer or rod. Listen for the ring. Higher-pitched ring = higher eigenfrequency. A limestone slab 30cm across will ring near 5,800 Hz. A granite block 50cm across rings near 5,500 Hz.

2

Measure the Frequency

Open the spectrum analyser app. Hold your phone 10–20cm from the stone. Strike again. The app shows the frequency spectrum — the tallest peak is the eigenfrequency. Write this number down. Strike several times to confirm it's consistent.

3

Match It With a Speaker

Set your tone generator to the measured frequency. Play it through your speaker aimed at the stone. Slowly sweep ±50 Hz around the measured value. At the exact eigenfrequency, the stone will vibrate visibly — place a few grains of sand on top and watch them dance when you hit the right note.

4

Compare to Theory

Calculate the predicted frequency: f = vsound / (2L). For granite use v = 5,500 m/s, for limestone v = 3,500 m/s. Measure L (longest dimension in metres). Compare your measured frequency to the prediction. They should match within ~20% (the stone isn't a perfect rectangular prism).

What You're Proving

Every stone has a specific, measurable, repeatable resonant frequency determined by its size and material. This is the frequency at which acoustic energy transfers most efficiently into the stone. Ancient masons who spent lifetimes working stone would have known this instinctively — the stone that "rings true" is the stone at eigenfrequency.

02

Speaker + Sand: See Standing Waves

Make the interference pattern visible. Chladni figures on a metal plate show you the nodes and antinodes.

Standing waves create fixed pressure nodes (quiet) and antinodes (loud). This is the mechanism behind the interference angle formula. In this experiment you make the pattern visible with sand on a vibrating plate.

🛒 Equipment

  • Metal plate (steel, brass, or aluminium, 20–40cm) ~$10
  • Speaker driver (bare driver, not in box) ~$15
  • Amplifier (any small amp or guitar amp) Existing
  • Fine sand or salt ~$3
  • Bolt, nut, rubber grommet ~$2
  • Tone generator app Free

⚡ How It Works

  • Speaker drives the plate at controlled frequency
  • At resonant modes, standing wave patterns form
  • Sand collects at the nodes (zero vibration)
  • Sand evacuates from antinodes (max vibration)
  • Result: beautiful geometric patterns = Chladni figures
Cross-section: speaker drives plate from below. Sand migrates to nodes. Changing frequency changes the pattern.
1

Mount the Plate

Bolt the centre of the metal plate to the speaker cone using a bolt through both, with a rubber grommet between. The speaker's vibration transfers directly into the plate. The plate should be free to vibrate at its edges — don't clamp them.

2

Sprinkle Sand, Sweep Frequency

Scatter fine sand evenly across the plate. Connect your tone generator → amplifier → speaker. Start at 200 Hz and slowly sweep upward. At each resonant mode, the sand will rapidly reorganise into a geometric pattern. These are Chladni figures — the visible geometry of standing waves.

3

Photograph Each Pattern and Note the Frequency

You'll find patterns at specific frequencies — typically 400–800 Hz for a 30cm steel plate, with increasingly complex patterns at higher modes. Record each. The first few modes show 2, 4, 6 node lines. Higher modes produce extraordinary geometric complexity.

4

Try Two Speakers (Advanced)

If you have a second speaker, place them at different positions under the plate. Drive both at the same frequency. The interference between the two sources creates a different pattern than either alone — you're now seeing the interference angle formula in action. Changing the spacing between speakers changes the pattern.

What You're Proving

Sound at specific frequencies creates fixed geometric pressure patterns. Nodes (zero vibration) and antinodes (maximum vibration) are positioned by the frequency and the geometry of the source(s). This is the mechanism by which ancient builders could position a pressure node at a specific point — the interference angle formula determines where.

03

Resonant Pipe Amplifier

Build a resonant chamber from PVC pipe. Measure the Q amplification directly.

A sealed pipe is a resonant chamber with measurable Q. Drive it at its eigenfrequency and measure the pressure amplification at the antinode. This demonstrates the chamber amplification principle — the same physics as the King's Chamber.

🛒 Equipment

  • PVC pipe, 50cm–1m length, 5–10cm diameter ~$8
  • PVC end cap (one end) ~$3
  • Small speaker driver (fits pipe diameter) ~$15
  • Amplifier Existing
  • SPL meter app (phone) Free
  • Tone generator Free

1

Assemble the Pipe

Seal one end with the PVC cap (glue or press-fit). Mount the speaker at the open end — it doesn't need to seal perfectly, just couple sound into the pipe. For a 50cm pipe, the fundamental resonance is at 172 Hz.

2

Measure Baseline: Speaker in Open Air

Play a 172 Hz tone through the speaker at a fixed volume. Hold your SPL meter app 5cm from the speaker. Record the dB reading. This is your baseline Pdrive.

3

Measure Amplified: At the Sealed End

Now play the same tone into the pipe. Hold your SPL meter right at the sealed end cap (or drill a small hole and insert a microphone). Sweep slowly around 172 Hz to find the exact peak. Record the dB reading at resonance.

4

Calculate Q

Q = 10(dB_resonant − dB_baseline) / 20. For a smooth PVC pipe you'll typically measure Q = 30–80. A smooth metal pipe or ceramic tube will give higher Q. A stone pipe or channel would give the highest — which is why ancient chambers, cut from solid stone, are such effective resonators.

✅
Try different pipe materials. PVC, metal, ceramic, even a rolled stone tile channel if you have one. You're directly measuring the quality factor that UFFT uses to calculate ancient chamber amplification. Stone gives the highest Q.

What You're Proving

A tube of the right length amplifies sound at its resonant frequency by a factor of Q. You're measuring the same principle that makes the King's Chamber (granite-lined, tuned dimensions) a powerful acoustic amplifier. Scale up the pipe to room-size, line it with stone, and Q reaches 100–500.

04

Ultrasonic Levitation

Levitate small objects using 40 kHz ultrasonic transducers. The direct demonstration.

This is the closest you can get at home to the full acoustic levitation mechanism. Off-the-shelf ultrasonic transducers at 40 kHz create standing wave nodes in air that physically trap and suspend small objects. Styrofoam beads, water droplets, and small insects levitate at the pressure nodes.

🛒 Equipment — Option A: Kit

  • TinyLev ultrasonic levitator kit ~$70
  • Includes transducers, driver board, frame
  • Arduino Nano (often included) —
  • USB power supply Existing
  • Styrofoam beads (1–3mm) ~$3

🛒 Equipment — Option B: DIY

  • 72× HC-SR04 ultrasonic transducers (40 kHz) ~$20
  • Arduino Nano ~$12
  • L298N motor driver board ~$8
  • 3D-printed or cardboard frame ~$5
  • Wiring, USB power ~$5

TinyLev arrangement: two opposing transducer arrays create standing wave. Objects levitate at pressure nodes spaced 4.3mm apart.
1

Build or Assemble the Levitator

If using the TinyLev kit, follow the included instructions — it's soldering 72 transducers to a driver board and mounting in a frame with two opposing arrays. The arrays face each other about 1–2cm apart. If DIY, the open-source TinyLev plans are freely available online.

2

Power Up and Insert Particles

Power the device via USB. Use tweezers or a fine tool to place styrofoam beads (1–3mm) into the gap. They snap to the pressure nodes and levitate in mid-air. Multiple objects will sit at different node layers, spaced 4.3mm apart (half the 40 kHz wavelength in air).

3

Test Different Objects

Try water droplets (use a wet toothpick), tiny ants or flies (they survive), small seeds, and coffee grounds. Maximum particle size is about 1.7mm (40% of the half-wavelength). Heavier objects require more transducer power — this is the direct relationship between acoustic pressure and levitation weight.

4

Observe the Node Structure

Multiple particles at different heights reveal the standing wave structure directly. The spacing between levitated layers is exactly λ/2 = 4.3mm. You're seeing the same physics that the interference angle formula describes — pressure nodes positioned by frequency and geometry.

🔬
The UFFT connection: This device levitates ~1mg objects using 72 tiny transducers. Scale the principle: more transducers (N² intensity scaling), lower frequency (larger objects), resonant enclosure (Q amplification), and geometric focusing. Every scaling factor is multiplicative. The ancient version uses hundreds of human voices instead of transducers, stone chambers instead of air gaps, and audible frequencies instead of ultrasonic.
05

Water Cavitation Cutting

Use an ultrasonic cleaner to demonstrate cavitation erosion on scored stone.

An off-the-shelf ultrasonic cleaner operates at 40 kHz — close to the Minnaert frequency for ~0.08mm cavitation bubbles. Submerge a scored piece of soft stone and observe erosion along the score line. This demonstrates the cavitation cutting mechanism from Section 7 of the working paper.

🛒 Equipment

  • Ultrasonic cleaner (40 kHz, 35W+) ~$30–60
  • Soft stone samples: soapstone, limestone, alabaster ~$10
  • Harder stone: marble or granite offcut ~$5
  • Scoring tool (nail, carbide scriber) ~$5
  • Magnifying glass or USB microscope ~$15
  • Ruler for measurement Existing

⚠️ Safety Notes

  • Never put hands in the cleaner while operating
  • Wear safety glasses when operating
  • Ultrasonic cleaners produce cavitation — the water may get warm
  • Work in a well-ventilated area
  • The 40 kHz sound is above hearing but can cause headaches at high power — don't lean over it
1

Prepare the Samples

Score a straight groove (~1mm deep) across each stone sample using the carbide scriber or a nail and hammer. Make the groove as consistent as possible. Also prepare a control sample: same stone, no groove. Photograph both under magnification before the experiment.

2

Submerge and Run

Fill the ultrasonic cleaner with water. Submerge the scored stone sample. Run for 30 minutes. The cleaner produces cavitation throughout the water — but cavitation concentrates at the groove (geometric stress concentration). Run the unscored control under identical conditions.

3

Measure and Compare

Remove both samples. Dry and photograph under magnification. Measure groove depth (if measurable) before vs after. On soft stone (soapstone, alabaster), you should see measurable deepening and widening of the groove while the unscored control shows minimal surface change. The scored groove concentrates cavitation — exactly the UFFT cutting mechanism.

4

Extended Run on Harder Stone

Repeat with limestone or marble. These require longer exposure — try 2–4 hours with periodic water changes (the water heats up). The effect is slower but the same mechanism applies. Cavitation erosion works on every material — it's a matter of time and power, not material hardness.

5

Add Abrasive (Advanced Enhancement)

Add a teaspoon of fine silicon carbide powder (available as lapidary grit, ~$5) to the water. The cavitation bubbles accelerate the abrasive particles to extreme velocities at the groove. This dramatically increases cutting rate — the ancient equivalent of sand + water + sound.

🔬
The physics: At 40 kHz, cavitation bubbles of radius ~0.08mm form and collapse 40,000 times per second. Each collapse generates a transient pressure jet estimated at 100 MPa – 1 GPa. Granite's tensile strength is 10 MPa. The cavitation jet exceeds the failure stress by 10–100×. The groove concentrates these events along the cut line — the same principle as a water-filled score channel driven by chanting at Minnaert frequency.

What You're Proving

Sound + water + a scored line = precision cutting through stone. The scored groove determines where the cut goes. The cavitation provides the GPa-level forces. The only input is electrical power to the ultrasonic transducer — or in the ancient case, acoustic power from coordinated human voices driving a water-filled channel at the right frequency. The hardness of the stone is irrelevant. Cavitation cuts everything.
